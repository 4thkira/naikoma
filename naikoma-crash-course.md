# Naikoma: A Crash Course

**Naikoma** (nai + ko + ma) means "love-home-tongue." It also means "the tongue of the absent home." Both readings are true at the same time. That ambiguity is the whole point of this language.

Naikoma is a constructed language with a small vocabulary and deep combination. Instead of having thousands of words, it has about 61 roots and 17 particles that carry multiple meanings each, and you build complex ideas by compounding and fusing them together. It's inspired by the philosophy of toki pona (radical simplicity, context-dependent meaning) and the layered readings you find across many languages — but its vocabulary echoes across many language families: Latin, Finnish, French, Korean, Greek, German, Arabic, and more. No single source dominates.

---

## The Two Registers

Naikoma has two "modes" of speaking, called **registers**. They change how the language sounds without changing the words themselves.

**Sacred/Formal register** is soft and open. Every syllable ends in a vowel. Vowels can be stretched long for emphasis. It sounds like a prayer or a lullaby. You'd use it for intimacy, ritual, poetry, or deep respect.

**Casual/Raw register** adds harder sounds — k, t, p, d, g, z, sh — and lets syllables close with consonants. It clips and snaps. You'd use it for everyday speech, urgency, rawness, or deliberate bluntness.

The same word shifts between them. **Nai** (love/absence) sounds like [naːi] drawn out and tender in sacred register, and [naik] with a sharp stop in casual. A speaker chooses how much register to apply — most real conversation lives somewhere in the middle, and the blending itself communicates. Using sacred words in casual structure says "I'm being real about something important." Using casual words in sacred structure says "I'm giving dignity to something raw."

Some roots belong more naturally to one register: **veri** (spring/truth) and **sola** (sun/alone) lean formal — they'd sound overly poetic in casual speech. **Nälk** (hunger) and **tume** (dark/shadow) lean casual — they'd sound crude in a prayer. Breaking these conventions is expressive, not wrong.

### Shifting Between Registers

Common strategies for moving a word from one register to another. None are mandatory — they're a toolkit, not rules. Mix and match as the moment demands.

**Sacred → Casual (hardening, closing):**
- **Truncation** — Drop the final vowel to close the syllable. lume → lum, ruta → rut, benu → ben. The word snaps shut.
- **Stop addition** — After truncating, add a hard stop: -k, -t, or -p. lume → lumk, tilu → tilk, nai → naik. -k is sharpest, -t is clipped, -p is blunt.
- **Consonant hardening** — Replace a soft consonant with a harder one. s → sh, h → k, v → g. Changes the texture without changing the structure. Use sparingly.
- **Vowel shortening** — Where sacred draws vowels long (naːi, tiluuu), casual snaps them short. Same letters, less breath. The lightest shift.

**Casual → Sacred (softening, opening):**
- **Vowel restoration** — Reopen a closed syllable by restoring or adding a final vowel. nälk → nälkä, lumk → lume. Restoring with ä instead of the original vowel adds formal color that wasn't there before.
- **Vowel lengthening** — Draw out vowels for weight and tenderness. demi → demii, dopu → dopuu. The word stretches, takes up more space, asks for more time.
- **Stop softening** — Drop or soften hard consonants. A final -k falls off, a cluster simplifies. naik → nai, lumk → lume. The hardness dissolves.
- **H-insertion** — Add a breathy /h/ between vowels or at syllable boundaries. Casual speech doesn't have time for breath; sacred speech makes room for it.

**Either direction:**
- **Register mixing** — Sacred roots in casual structure, or vice versa. The mismatch IS the meaning.
- **Partial shift** — Only shift part of a compound, leaving the rest in its natural register. One half tender, one half hard. The seam between registers inside a single word is itself expressive.

## All 61 Roots

Each root carries multiple meanings, and each echoes words from several real languages — not copied from any one, but shaped so that speakers of different languages might hear ghosts in the sounds.

### Feeling & Connection

- **nai** — love, devotion, bond, affirmation; also absence, without, not (as suffix). Echoes GR ναι (yes), FI nainen (woman), LA nativus (born). Says "yes" and "not" at the same time.
- **kore** — heart, core, inside, essence, truth. Echoes EN core, FR cœur, LA cor, GR korē (the person reflected in someone's pupil).
- **nälk** — hunger, wanting, raw need. Echoes FI nälkä (hunger). Guttural and uncomfortable. Casual register.
- **vole** — want, intend, choose, will. Echoes LA velle, FR vouloir, IT volere, EN volition. The deliberate kind of wanting (vs. nälk's craving).

### People & Self

- **mi** — I, me, my, we. Echoes EN me, ES mí, IT mi, LA mihi. Universal first-person.
- **si** — you, your. Echoes FI sinä (you), ES/PT si (self), FR si (so/if), IT sì (yes).
- **ta** — they, them, that one, it. Echoes RU та/то (that), HI तो (then), ZH 他 tā (he/she), EN that.
- **ani** — person, being, someone, soul. Echoes LA anima (soul), HE אני ani (I), AR أنا anā (I). "Person" echoes "I" in Semitic — every person is a self.
- **valo** — life, alive, creature, worth. Echoes FI valo (light), ES valor (value), LA valere (to be strong), FR valoir (to be worth).

### Place & Belonging

- **ko** — home, hearth, belonging, small familiar thing. Echoes FI koti, EN cottage, HI kuṭī, KR 고 (place-suffix).
- **selu** — earth, ground, soil, solid, foundation. Echoes LA solum (ground), FR sol (floor), EN soil, FI selkä (ridge).
- **mere** — sea, depth, origin, vastness. Echoes FR mer, FI meri, EN mere, LA mare. Formal register.
- **ruta** — path, way, road, method, how. Echoes LA/ES ruta, FR route, EN route, DE Rute (rod).
- **ma** — tongue, word, speech; also land, ground, place. Echoes the mama/mouth pattern, KR 말 (speech), HU ma (today), TP ma (land).

### Light, Fire & Dark

- **lume** — fire, light, burning, will, illumination. Echoes LA lumen, FR lumière, IT lume, RO lumea (world — the lit place).
- **sola** — light, star, sun; also alone, solitary. Echoes LA sol, ES/IT sola (alone). The sun is always alone. Formal register.
- **tume** — dark, shadow, hidden, unknown, buried. Echoes FI tumma, LA tumulus (grave), EN tomb, FR tombe. Casual register.

### Change & Time

- **veri** — spring, renewal, becoming, truth; also in-progress (as suffix). Echoes FI veri (blood/life), LA veritas, FR vert (green), IT vero. Formal register.
- **ruma** — decay, ruin, rest, ending; also completed (as suffix). Echoes LA ruina, FI ruma, DE Raum (space — what remains), SE rum.
- **novi** — fresh, new, different, change. Echoes LA novus, RU новый, FR nouveau, IT nuovo, HR novi.
- **hora** — time, moment, season, cycle, when. Echoes LA/ES/IT hora, GR hōra (hour/season), FR heure.

### Perception & Knowledge

- **mira** — mirror, eye, seeing, wonder. Echoes LA mirari (wonder), ES mirar (look), FR miroir, EN admire.
- **savi** — know, understand, wise, aware. Echoes FR savoir, LA sapere (to be wise/taste), ES saber, IT sapere, FI savi (tone).
- **sana** — name, word, identity, essence, wholeness. Echoes FI sana (word), LA sanus (healthy), TR san (title).

### Body & Threshold

- **vena** — blood, body, vital force, flow, connection. Echoes LA/ES/IT/FR vena/veine (vein). Blood as what connects.
- **ora** — prayer, plea, mouth, edge, threshold. Echoes LA orare (pray), IT ora (now), FR or (gold), LA ōra (shore).
- **mano** — hand, arm, touch, hold, do-by-hand. Echoes LA manus, ES/IT mano, FR main. The hand across Romance.
- **kapu** — head, top, mind, thought, to think. Echoes LA caput, IT capo, DE Kopf, EN cap/capital.
- **hava** — air, breath, wind, spirit, weather. Echoes TR hava (air), AR hawā' (air), HE havah (breath), EN heaven (distant).
- **moku** — eat, food, consume, sustain, taste. Echoes Hawaiian/Maori moku (to bite/take in), TP moku, EN munch (distant).
- **avu** — water, liquid, drink, rain, to flow. Echoes LA aqua (a-u frame), FR eau, AR mā' (water), Tamil āvu (steam).

### Intimacy

- **tena** — skin, flesh, tenderness, to hold close, physical intimacy. Echoes TR ten (skin), LA tenēre (hold close), IT tenero (tender), FR tendre, FI tenho (charm). Tenderness and skin share a root across families.
- **siru** — inner knowing, soul-intimacy, co-presence, the thread between minds. The closeness that doesn't need a body. Echoes AR sirr (secret), JP 知る shiru (to know deeply), KR 실 sil (thread), FI siru (fragment — what you share of yourself).

### Living Things

- **beru** — animal, beast, creature-with-will, wild thing. Echoes EN bear, RU зверь zver' (wild animal), LA fera (wild creature — f/b bilabial swap), DE Bär, FI peto (beast). Sounds like something with a ribcage.
- **hana** — plant, flower, growing-rooted thing, bloom. Echoes JP 花 hana (flower), KR 하나 hana (one/first — the first things that grew), AR ḥinnā' (henna — plant-dye). The bloom as synecdoche for all rooted life.
- **mori** — fungus, mycelium, the decomposer, the hidden network. Echoes JP 森 mori (forest — where fungi live), LA mori (to die — what fungi process), IT morbido (soft), EN memory (distant). Forest and death in the same mouth.

### Face & Surface

- **sura** — face, mask, expression, emotion-as-displayed. The surface you show others; what you choose to wear or can't help revealing. Echoes AR ṣūra (image/form), HI sūrat (face/appearance), Swahili sura (face/chapter of a life), Amharic sura (picture). Three unrelated families — Semitic, Indo-Aryan, Bantu. Near-rhyme with **suri** (big/great) — your face is the biggest thing others see of you.

### Stone & Hardness

- **kalu** — stone, rock, hardness, rigidity, permanence; also weapon, the thing that endures, the thing that kills. Echoes Tamil kal (stone), FI kallo (skull), Sanskrit kāla (time/death — stone and death share permanence), Welsh calch (limestone). Carries the memory of Cain's weapon — the first murder was a stone. Thematic pair with **kapu** (head/skull): the skull and the stone that broke it.

### Color & Appearance

- **oku** — color, hue, appearance, the surface a thing shows you. Echoes Yoruba àwọ̀ (color/skin/surface), Maori āko (to learn/reflect), KR 옥 ok (jade/gem — colored stone), and the arc/ark rainbow pattern across FR arc-en-ciel, Basque arku (arch/bow). Used as a marker to specify color: oku-vena = red, oku-mere = deep blue, oku-hana = green. Without oku, the root just means itself — vena is blood, not "red."

### Pain & Healing

- **dopu** — pain, hurt, suffering, ache; the raw immediate sensation, physical and emotional as one. Echoes LA dolor (pain/grief), FR douleur (suffering), IT dolore, FI kipu (pain), EN pain (from LA poena). The /d/ from dolore, the /pu/ from kipu. Sounds like a dull hit. Distinct from malu (the state of brokenness), tume (grief), reka (rage) — dopu is the moment of hurting itself.
- **kasu** — healing, mending, restoration, tending, recovery; the process of becoming whole. Echoes GR Asklepios/Asclepius (god of healing — k-s frame rearranged), LA caduceus (the healer's staff), FI kasvaa (to grow — healing as growth), EN cure. Mythological weight, not clinical — the snake-staff implies healing involves transformation, not just gentleness. Can heal what was never wounded: tending a garden, raising a child.

### Obsession & Spiral

- **viru** — obsession, fixation, spiral, tightening repetition, the pull toward a center; coil, vortex. Echoes LA vīrus (venom — what spirals through the body), FI virta (current/stream), Swahili viringa (to go around), EN veer/whirl. Near-rhyme with **veri** (spring/becoming) is deliberate: growth and obsession are the same motion at different speeds. One spirals outward, the other in.
- **demi** — dominance, submission, the power dynamic itself; to hold authority, to yield authority; the exchange of power between selves. Context determines which side you're hearing: the dominant says "I hold," the submissive says "I yield." Same word, same act. Echoes LA dominari (to rule), dominus (lord), from domus (house/home) — authority rooted in the household. FR demi (half) — the dynamic is always half of something. The **mi** inside echoes Naikoma's own mi (I/self) — whether holding or yielding, there's a self at the center.

### Protection & Shelter

- **ramu** — to protect, to shield, to guard, to stand between; defense, shelter, armor; the relationship between protector and protected. Echoes Sanskrit rakṣ (to protect), JP 守る mamoru (to protect), AR ḥamā (to guard), EN rampart (defensive wall), LA arma (arms/armor — r-m reversed). In sacred, tender and encompassing; in casual, martial and hard. Compounds: ramu-le (guardian), ramu-tena (armor — protection-skin), ramu-kalu (shield — protection-stone).

### Freedom & Bondage

- **luka** — freedom, bondage, the tether itself; the state of being bound or being free; chain, leash, liberation, release. Polysemous like nai — the one who holds the chain says "I bind," the one who chooses to stay says "I am free." Same word, same act. Echoes GR λύω lýō (to free — lu- onset), LA ligāre (to bind — l- carries both directions), FI lukko (lock — bondage that opens with a key), Malay luka (wound — where the chain meets skin). Parallels demi: both relational, both hold two sides of a dynamic. Compounds: luka-le (one in the tether), luka-nai (unbound/the love inside freedom), luka-demi (the binding power dynamic).

### Dream & Desire

- **halu** — dream, desire, vision, longing, other-place. Echoes FI halu (desire), DE Hall (echo), AR ḥulm (dream), EN hallucinate. Formal register.

### Silence & Stillness

- **tilu** — silence, stillness, space, peace; quiet violence (casual). Echoes FI tila (space), EN still, ES tranquilo (distant).

### Quality & Description

- **benu** — good, right, well, kind, correct. Echoes LA bene, FR bien, IT bene, EN benign/benefit.
- **malu** — bad, wrong, broken, harmful, ill. Echoes LA malus, FR mal, ES mal, EN malice.
- **suri** — big, great, important, much, vast. Echoes FI suuri (big), LA super- (above), HI sūraj (sun), FR sur.
- **lili** — small, little, few, young, simple. Echoes TP lili, EN little. The /li/ sound phonesthetically means "small" across languages.

### Ability & Action

- **pode** — can, able, possible, power, permission. Echoes ES/PT poder, IT potere, LA posse, FR pouvoir.
- **tava** — go, move, travel, toward, away. Echoes TP tawa, FI tavata (meet), HU tova (onward), PT vai (go!).
- **pali** — do, make, create, work, act. Echoes GR páli (again), FI pala (piece/what's made), Sanskrit phala (result), TP pali.
- **volu** — give, offer, share, release. Echoes LA voluntas (will), FR vouloir, IT volere, EN voluntary — giving as willing.
- **kutu** — to use, to employ, to instrumentalize; making something serve a purpose; the relationship between user and used. Echoes TR kullanmak (to use), FI käyttää (to use), Swahili -tumia (to use), LA ūtī/ūsus (to use), JP 使う tsukau. Polysemous: in sacred register, deliberate and transactional (a tool respected); in casual, exploitative (a thing wrung dry). Central to Konoko's philosophy: **nai li kutu** — "love is use."

### Things & Stuff

- **resa** — thing, object, matter, stuff, situation. Echoes LA res (thing), IT/ES cosa (from LA causa), FR chose, EN real.

### Togetherness

- **semu** — together, with, among; also apart, without (polysemy!). Echoes LA simul (together), GR syn- (s-m frame), FR ensemble, PT sem (without!) — together and without share a root.

### Numbers

- **wan** — one, single, unit. Echoes EN one/wan, TP wan, DE wann (when — the single moment), ZH 万 wàn (ten thousand — ironic flip from one to myriad).
- **du** — two, pair, both, duality. Echoes LA duo, EN dual/two, DE du (you — the second person!), RU два, GR dýo.
- **muta** — many, much, several, abundance; very (intensifier). Echoes LA multus, EN multitude, TP mute, FI muta (mud — what accumulates).
- **ale** — all, everything, complete, whole, always. Echoes TP ale, DE alle, EN all, AR al-kull (everything), GR holos (whole).

## Particles

Seventeen small words that hold sentences together:

- **wa** — topic marker: "as for..." *(nai wa veri — "as for love, it's spring")*
- **de** — possessive, "of/from": *(kore de lume — "fire's heart")*
- **al** — direction, toward, for: *(tava al ko — "going home")*
- **e** — object marker: *(mi e lume mira — "I see fire")*
- **la** — context/condition, "if/when": *(lume la, kore li veri — "if fire, the heart renews")*
- **li** — links subject to what it is/does: *(ko li nai — "home is love")*
- **ne** — seeking agreement: *(benu ne? — "it's good, right?")*
- **ka** — question: *(si li benu ka — "are you well?")*
- **va** — emphasis/assertion: *(mi li savi va — "I know, I tell you")*
- **pri** — before, earlier, prior to: *(pri mi tava, si e ko mira — "before I leave, look at home")*
- **pos** — after, following, then: *(pos hora-ruma, tilu — "after the ending, silence")*
- **kin** — because, therefore, so: *(mi li tava, kin ko li ruma — "I leave, because home is ruined")*
- **pe** — on, above, upon, over: *(key li pe table — "the key is on the table")*
- **so** — under, below, beneath: *(box li so counter — "the box is under the counter")*
- **ni** — in, inside, at, within: *(avu li ni mug — "water is in the mug")*
- **fo** — in front of, facing: *(mi li fo ko — "I'm in front of the house")*
- **do** — behind, at the back of: *(ta li do kalu-selu — "they're behind the wall")*

## Basic Grammar

**Word order** is Subject-Object-Verb. "I see fire" = **mi e lume mira** (I [obj] fire see).

**Any root can be a noun, verb, or adjective.** Lume = fire (noun), to burn (verb), burning (adjective). Context and particles clarify.

**Negation** uses **nai** as a suffix, creating intentional ambiguity. **Mira nai** = "unseen / to not see" AND "to see love / to see absence." Both at once.

**Aspect** uses root-suffixes:
- **-veri** = becoming, in progress *(mira-veri = "starting to see")*
- **-ruma** = completed *(mira-ruma = "done seeing" / "gone blind")*
- **-nälk** = about to, wanting to *(mira-nälk = "wanting to see")*

No tense. No plural marking. Context handles both.

**Modifiers** are small affixes that transform root meanings. Five particles, hundreds of new forms:

- **su-** (prefix) = very, extremely, most. **Su-lume** = blazing. **Su-nälk** = ravenous. **Su-tilu** = absolute silence.
- **re-** (prefix) = again, back, returning. **Re-tava** = to return. **Re-ko** = homecoming. **Re-mira** = to look again.
- **-nai** (suffix) = opposite, without, un-. **Lume-nai** = cold/dark. **Benu-nai** = unwell. **Tilu-nai** = noise. Still polysemous — lume-nai is "without fire" AND "fire's absence."
- **-le** (suffix) = one who, agent, -er. **Mira-le** = seer. **Savi-le** = sage. **Tilu-le** = one who keeps silence.
- **-vu** (suffix) = -able, -worthy, capable of being. **Nai-vu** = lovable. **Mira-vu** = worth seeing. **Siru-vu** = someone you could share a soul with. Always an invitation, never a demand.

- **-ovi** (suffix) = reversed, backwards, inverted. **Tava-ovi** = going backwards. **Hora-ovi** = time reversed. **Viru-ovi** = obsession unwinding. Distinct from re- (repeat) and -nai (negate) — -ovi plays the same thing in the other direction.

Modifiers stack: **su-nai-vu** = "most worthy of love." **Re-tilu-le-veri** = "becoming again one who keeps silence." Order: prefix + root + semantic suffix + aspect suffix.

## Example Sentences

Here's what Naikoma sounds like in practice:

**Basic conversation:**
- **mi li benu** — "I'm good."
- **si li benu ka** — "Are you good?"
- **mi e moku vole** — "I want food."
- **mi li tava al ko** — "I'm going home."
- **si e resa pali ka** — "Are you making something?"
- **ta li tilu** — "They're quiet." / "They are silence."

**More complex:**
- **hora de ruma la, mi li tilu** — "When it's the time of endings, I'm silent."
- **mi e si savi nai** — "I don't know you." / "I know you through absence."
- **avu li tava, selu li moku** — "Water flows, earth consumes."
- **muta ani li ko al tava** — "Many people are going home."
- **mano de si li lume, ne** — "Your hands are fire, right?"

**Poetic/layered:**
- **mi wa, kore li nälk, kapu li halu** — "As for me: the heart hungers, the mind dreams."
- **semu wa nai, semu wa nai nai** — "Togetherness is love; togetherness is also the absence of love."
- **ale hora la, valo li veri** — "In all seasons, life is becoming."
- **ruta al ko li mere** — "The road home is an ocean."

## Compounding

Stack roots together. The **last root is the head** (what the thing IS). Earlier roots modify it.

- **nai-ko** (love + home) = "a home made of love"
- **ko-nai** (home + love) = "the love one has for home" — homesickness
- **lume-kore** (fire + heart) = "a passionate heart"
- **kore-lume** (heart + fire) = "the fire from within"

Some useful compounds:

- **tilu-ruma** (silence + rest) = "sleep"
- **selu-ko** (earth + home) = "building, house"
- **beru-mere** (beast + sea) = "sea creature"
- **beru-hava** (beast + air) = "bird, flying creature"
- **selu-hana** (earth + plant) = "tree, deeply rooted plant"
- **mori-selu** (fungus + earth) = "mycelium, underground network"
- **benu-kore** (good + heart) = "joy, happiness"
- **nai-ruma** (love + ended) = "grief, sadness"
- **lume-kore** (fire + heart) = "anger, passion"
- **tume-kore** (dark + heart) = "fear"
- **halu-ruma** (dream + ended) = "memory"
- **tume-sana** (shadow + word) = "lie"
- **hava-ma** (breath + word) = "song, music"
- **semu-ani** (together + person) = "friend"
- **valo-ruma** (life + ended) = "death"

## Fusion

This is where Naikoma gets really interesting. Compounding is transparent — anyone can read it. Fusion compresses multiple roots into single words that can be as clear or as opaque as you choose.

**Intentional substitution:** using one root where a different one is expected, because the gap IS the meaning. Saying **nai de lume** ("my love's fire") instead of **nai de kore** ("my love's heart") — because this love isn't gentle. The substitution only works when the listener can feel the ghost of the word that *should* be there.

**Clipping** is the key to short words and names. Any root can be reduced to its first consonant + vowel: lume → **lu**, kore → **kor**, mira → **mir**, tume → **tu**, halu → **hal**. Monosyllabic roots (nai, ko, ma, mi, si) are already clips. This lets you build tight 1-3 syllable fusions:

- **lu** + **mir** → **Lumir** (2 syllables — "fire-seeing")
- **kor** + **nai** → **Kornai** (2 syllables — "heart-love / heart-absence")
- **tu** + **lu** → **Tulu** (2 syllables — "shadow-fire")
- Or just a bare clip as a name: **Lu** (1 syllable — fire, distilled to a spark)

**Blending** merges two roots into one word. When roots share sounds at the boundary, they overlap: **mira** + **ruma** = **miruma** (the R bridges — "watching something end"). Can use full roots or clips.

**Deep fusion** collapses 3+ roots into one word. With clips, you can keep it short:

- **lu** + **nä** + **kor** → **Lunäkor** (3 syllables from 3 roots — "burning hunger in the heart")
- **nai** + **ko** + **ma** → **naikoma** (3 syllables — the language's own name)
- **hal** + **ve** + **nai** → **Halvnai** (2 syllables — "the truth dreamt by love")

The degree of compression controls who can read it. Transparent fusions can be parsed by anyone who knows the roots. Opaque ones need the speaker to explain them.

## Building Names

A Naikoma name is a compressed sentence. Up to four positions:

```
[Role]  [Personal Name]  [Affiliation]  [Secret]
```

**Role** — what you are, stated with intention. Using **nälk** (hunger) means "I am defined by what I lack." Using **tilu** (silence) means "I'm defined by what I don't say." Can be a clip: **Lu** (fire) as a role = "I am a spark."

**Personal Name** — a fusion capturing who you are. Can be anything from a single clip (**Mir** — seeing) to a deep fusion (**Lunäkor** — fire-hunger-heart). The densest part.

**Affiliation** — the group/family name. Everyone in the group shares it. A compressed manifesto.

**Secret** — optional, shared only with trusted people. Always in sacred register. The language insists.

Not every slot needs filling. A name can be as short as one syllable.

**Short name examples:**
- **Nai** — just the word for love/absence. A complete name.
- **Kornai** — heart-love. Two syllables, two roots.
- **Lu Naiko** — fire [role], love-home [personal]. Five syllables total across two positions.
- **Solu Halvnai** — sun-fire [role], dream-truth-love [personal]. Four syllables, five roots.

**Long name example for contrast:**
- **Ora Tunäkore Navekome** — prayer/threshold [role], dark-hunger-heart [personal], love-truth-home-sea [affiliation, opaque]. Twelve syllables, a full manifesto.

The literal root-parsing and the speaker's own translation are both valid. A name lives in the gap between those two readings.

## Loanwords

Naikoma is an intimacy language, not a universal one. For modern, manufactured, or highly specific objects — things that don't carry emotional or philosophical weight — English loanwords are perfectly natural. A speaker might say **"mi e box tume-ko la volu-ruma"** ("I left the box in the storage room") without any loss of authenticity. The loanword sits inside Naikoma grammar, taking particles and word order like any root.

The boundary is intuitive: if a concept is ancient, felt, or relationally meaningful (face, stone, fire, love, hunger), it belongs in Naikoma. If it's a specific modern object (battery, flashlight, jacket, toaster), English handles it. The language doesn't need to name everything — it needs to name everything that *matters*.

## A Note on Gaps

There are still gaps — no dedicated words for "rain," "trust," "teach," or dozens of other concepts. Most of these can be compounded from what exists (rain = avu-tava, "water that moves"; trust = benu-savi, "good knowing"; bird = beru-hava, "beast of air"). And when compounding falls short, new roots can be coined — drawing echoes from three or more unrelated languages until the word sounds like it belongs to all of them and none. (The newest root, **kutu** — to use — was coined to fill a gap that became impossible to ignore when the world's central philosophy needed it.)

The language grows from use. Every gap is a door.
